The Wonderful Czech Republic

  • Prague The Czech capital and one of Europe's most romantic cities, boasting a remarkably well-preserved Old Town with top-notch architecture from every era; a sprawling hilltop castle; a thriving New Town packed with slinky Art Nouveau facades; a historic and well-presented Jewish Quarter; inspiring tales from its rocky late-20th-century history; countless lively pubs serving some of the world's best beer; and enough museums,
  • Český Krumlov Charming, picturesque, and popular midsized town huddled under a colorful castle and hugging a river bend in the Bohemian hills, made to order for canoeing, hiking, and people-watching.
  • Kutná Hora Finely preserved yet down-to-earth city, once home to the world's largest silver mine, now best known for an opulent cathedral — built with riches from the mining bonanza — and an evocative chapel decorated with human bones.
  • Terezín Memorial Walled town and fortress near Prague that served as a WWII internment camp and Gestapo prison (respectively), both now housing poignant memorials.
  • Telč Tranquil day-trip stop (or good home base for Moravia and southern Bohemia) known for its enormous fairy-tale square and lavish Renaissance castle.
  • Třeboň Bustling Bohemian spa town surrounded by artificial lakes — ideal for biking, wandering, and sampling the local fish.
  • Mucha's Slav Epic Monumental series of paintings that's worth the trek to time-forgotten (and otherwise skippable) town of Moravský Krumlov.
  • Konopiště Castle Former residence of the Habsburg Archduke Franz Ferdinand and his Czech bride, with the most lived-in-feeling interior (and most interesting historical tour) of any Czech castle (easy day trip from Prague).
  • Karlštejn Castle Popular castle — built by Charles IV just outside Prague — with perhaps the country's most dramatic exterior.
  • Třebíč Scenic riverside burg worth a few hours for its large Romanesque basilica and well-preserved jewish Quarter popular with artists. with echoes of Jewish history; and — huddled along the Austrian border — Slavonice, with hiking trails, an artistic community and evocative, never-used WWII fortifications in the nearby forests.
  • Slavonice Dreamy artists' retreat tucked near the Austrian border that harbors evocative, never-used WWII fortifications in nearby forests.
  • Karlovy Vary (Carlsbad) Genteel, architecture-packed spa town where well-heeled Russian and German tourists stroll manicured riverfront promenades sipping icky (but healthy) spring water.
  • Olomouc Moravian cultural capital and thriving university city, with a grand square (crowned by Europe's biggest plague column), churches, museums, famously pungent cheese, bustling everyday life. Nearby is the country's top Rococo château in Kroměříž.
  • Wallachia Time-passed, mountainous corner of Moravia, with cute villages, open-air folk museums at Rožnov pod Radhoštěm, and a mountaintop retreat at Pustevny.
  • Wallachia Křivoklát Castle Genuinely Gothic hunting palace of Czech kings — rustic, simple, and set in a forest — that was later transformed into a royal prison, now with a lively workshop courtyard in summer.
  • Mikulov Wine Region The Czech Republic's top wine-growing area, with a trio of worthwhile stops within a few minutes' drive: the historical town of Mikulov (postcard-perfect main square and vibrant Jewish sites), humble village of Pavlov (lots of wineries open for tastings), and impressive château-and-garden complexes of Lednice (more interesting) and Valtice.
